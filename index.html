03☀ Sun Safety — Planning Around Abigail

Why Japan in summer actually works for her, what to bring, and what to buy in Tokyo.

The core insight: Japan's sun-protection culture is mainstream and fashion-forward in a way the US's isn't. UV umbrellas (higasa), arm sleeves, wide-brim hats, and UV-cut clothing are everyday Japanese summer wear, sold at every department store and convenience store. For a teenager who is highly UV-sensitive, this means her UV gear becomes vacation accessories, not medical equipment. She blends with the cultural aesthetic instead of standing out.

Why early June works for her

  • Cloud cover. June has Tokyo's lowest sunshine of the year. ~27% of days are sunny; the rest are overcast or mostly overcast. [Japan-Guide / JMA] Cloud cover doesn't eliminate UV (up to 80% penetrates) but it dramatically reduces direct exposure hours.
  • Cooler temperatures (~68–80°F). Long sleeves, arm sleeves, wide-brim hats, and UV-blocking layers are wearable without heat stress. By July or August at 86°F+ with high humidity, the protective layer becomes a heat-illness risk stacked on top of the UV problem. [Japansophy]
  • No competing tradeoff. July is hotter AND has higher UV. Post-July 20 is even worse: peak Japanese crowds + peak heat + UV 10–11. June is the optimum across all dimensions.

04Safety Tips

What's actually risky in Japan vs. what isn't — the "dangerous neighborhoods" frame is mostly wrong here, but a handful of real tourist-specific risks deserve attention.

The big-picture reframe: Japan has some of the lowest violent crime rates of any major tourist destination, and Tokyo and Kyoto are particularly safe. There is no equivalent of the "skip this neighborhood" warning you'd give for parts of Paris, Rome, or major US cities. The actual tourist risks in Japan are different in character: heat illness, earthquake/tsunami protocol, location-specific scams in nightlife districts, and a few enforceable etiquette rules (notably Gion photo restrictions). This section covers those.

1. Heat illness — the single biggest medical risk in June

Japan summer heat is genuinely dangerous for the unacclimatized, and June marks the start of the high-risk window. The numbers from recent years are staggering:

  • June 2025 alone: 17,229 heatstroke cases nationwide — double June 2024. [Japan Living Life 2026]
  • May–September 2024: a record 97,578 emergency transports for heatstroke nationwide; 120 fatalities before reaching medical care; ~60% of patients were seniors but ~30% were adults 18–64. [Nippon.com / JMA]
  • The Tokyo Fire Department reports the trend is accelerating year-over-year as average summer temperatures rise. [This Is Japan 2025]

For your June 5–14 trip specifically

  • The UV-aware scheduling in Sun Safety (early morning + indoor midday + evening) is also the heat-illness-aware schedule. The two strategies overlap almost entirely.
  • Check WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature) heat alerts daily. Japan's heatstroke alert system runs late April through October; alerts trigger at WBGT 33+. Available in the JNTO Safety Tips app and via the Ministry of Environment's heat illness prevention website. [Nippon.com]
  • Hydrate aggressively. Vending machines on every corner sell Pocari Sweat (electrolyte drink), salt tablets (shio-ame), and water. Use them constantly, not optionally.
  • Recognize symptoms early: dizziness, nausea, heavy sweating, headache. Get to AC and water within minutes. Severe heatstroke (confusion, no sweating, very high body temp) is a medical emergency — call 119.
  • Convenience stores and department stores function as informal cooling shelters during alerts — duck inside any time you feel the heat building.

2. Earthquake and tsunami protocol

Most earthquakes in Japan are small and brief. Modern buildings are engineered to sway, not collapse. But the protocol is worth knowing cold before you arrive — it's the one situation where a few seconds of correct action genuinely matters.

The single most important thing to do before arrival

Download the JNTO "Safety Tips" app on every family member's iPhone. It's a free Japan Tourism Agency app that pushes earthquake early warnings, tsunami warnings, weather alerts, and heatstroke alerts in 15+ languages including English. Also includes an evacuation flowchart, helpful Japanese phrases for emergencies, and links to disaster information. [JNTO official] [App Store]

During an earthquake: Drop, Cover, Hold On

  • Indoors: Drop to hands and knees, get under a sturdy table or desk, cover your head and neck with your arms, hold on until shaking stops. Stay away from windows. Do NOT use elevators. Do NOT run outside during shaking — falling debris outside is more dangerous than well-engineered modern interiors. [Japan Cultural Journal 2026] [Euronews / JNTO]
  • Outdoors: Move to an open area away from buildings, power lines, and trees. If no open area, use a backpack or bag to protect your head and neck.
  • On a train: The Shinkansen and most JR/metro lines have automatic seismic braking (UrEDAS) that stops trains the moment sensors detect a threshold tremor. Stay seated, hold a handrail or strap, follow crew instructions. The abrupt stop is the safety feature working. [JRPass]
  • In a hotel: Check evacuation route postings in your room when you check in — standard in Japanese hotels. Hotels post the location of the nearest hinanjo (evacuation center, marked with 避難所 / a roof pictogram on blue-and-white signs).

Tsunami protocol

Neither Tokyo nor Kyoto is at meaningful tsunami risk for the places you'll be. Tokyo's downtown core sits at elevation, and Kyoto is inland. The protocol matters only if you make a coastal day trip. If you ever feel a strong earthquake near the coast: go higher immediately — uphill, or up into a "tsunami tower" (marked with blue evacuation signs). Don't wait for an official warning if shaking near the coast was strong. Cars get stuck in traffic; on foot is faster. Stay at high ground until the all-clear — secondary waves often follow the first.

3. Gion photo enforcement (Kyoto) — real fines, easy to avoid

Kyoto's Gion district has enforced photography restrictions on private streets since 2019, with a stricter expansion in April 2024. The fine is up to ¥10,000 (~$70) for unauthorized entry or photography on marked private roads. The Gion South Side District Council (residents and shop owners) drove the rules in response to tourist behavior: chasing geiko (Kyoto's geisha) and maiko (apprentices) down alleys, trespassing on private property, demanding selfies. Video surveillance is installed on the private roads. [AFAR] [Time Out / Japan Times]

4. Tokyo nightlife district scams — Kabukicho and Roppongi

Japanese and English-language safety guides agree: Kabukicho (Shinjuku) and Roppongi are physically safe, financially dangerous. Random violence against tourists is rare. The real risk is a category of scams called bottakuri (ぼったくり) — extortion via outrageous bar charges, sometimes with physical intimidation, occasionally with drink spiking. The US Embassy in Tokyo has issued repeat advisories specifically about both districts. [Gen Z Japan 2026] [Tokyo Insider Guide 2026] [Tokyo Paladin 2025]

The one rule that prevents 99% of trouble: never follow a street tout (kyakuhiki, 客引き). Reputable Japanese businesses do not need touts. Anyone aggressively flagging you down to a bar, club, or restaurant — including ones claiming to be staff for famous chains like Torikizoku or Hub — is leading you to a rip-off establishment. Touting is technically illegal in many Tokyo entertainment districts; the businesses that openly use touts are almost universally scams. [Japan Today 2026]

Specific scam patterns to know

  • The "cheap drink" / "sister shop" hook. Tout offers all-you-can-drink for ¥2,000, or claims the famous chain you were headed to is full and there's a "sister branch" nearby. Once inside: surprise cover charges, ice fees, weekend fees, mandatory drinks for staff, ¥50,000+ bills, intimidation to pay. [One Day Away 2026]
  • Drink spiking + credit card fraud. Less common but documented, mostly in deep Kabukicho back alleys and parts of Roppongi. Police involvement is often slow and unsatisfying for foreigners. Prevention is upstream — don't enter the establishment. [Tokyo Paladin]
  • Physical confinement / forced payment. In severe cases tourists report being physically blocked from leaving until they pay. Rare but real. The point of entry is the tout; everything downstream is harder to escape than the initial "no thanks."

What's safe to walk through

  • The well-lit main streets of Kabukicho — Central Road, the area around the Godzilla Head / Tokyu Kabukicho Tower, Omoide Yokocho (the yakitori alley already in the Food section) — are fine, including at night, including with the family. Visually intense but not dangerous.
  • Reputable restaurants and izakayas with posted prices, menus visible from the street, and no tout outside.
  • Bars booked through a hotel concierge or a reputable reservation platform.

Fake monks — Asakusa, Ueno, Harajuku, Akihabara

A separate, lower-stakes-but-persistent scam: people in orange robes (not actual monks) approach tourists near Senso-ji, Ueno Park, Meiji Shrine, and around Harajuku and Akihabara stations. They press a "gift" — prayer beads, an amulet, a metal medallion — into your hand or onto your wrist, ask you to sign a "donation book" showing previous tourists giving ¥1,000–10,000, then demand a matching donation. If you refuse, they sometimes follow and shout. Real Buddhist monks do not approach strangers for donations. [Japan Inside 2026] [SoraNews24]

If approached: don't accept the item, keep walking, say "no" firmly. If they put a bracelet on your wrist anyway, hand it back and walk away. The kids should know this pattern — fake monks specifically target obvious tourists, including teens.

5. Trains — etiquette, women-only cars, chikan

Women-only cars (relevant for Shaina, Abigail, Isabelle)

Women-only train cars (josei senyō sharyō, 女性専用車両) exist on many Tokyo and Osaka commuter lines, primarily to prevent chikan (痴漢, groping) during crowded commutes. Marked with bright pink signs on the platform and on the car doors. [Metropolis Japan 2026] [Dana Yao 2026]

  • Hours vary by line. Most commonly weekday morning rush (~7:30–9:30am) on heavy commuter lines: JR Saikyo, Chuo, Sobu, and select periods on Yamanote; Tokyo Metro lines variously. [Medium / Yamashita Guild]
  • Boys and men should not board these cars during enforced hours. Confrontation is unlikely (Japanese culture avoids it) but it can result in stares or someone notifying staff. Outside enforced hours, the car is open to all.
  • Your itinerary doesn't lean on rush-hour commuter trains, so this is good-to-know rather than essential. For Shaina, Abigail, and Isabelle on any split-from-the-boys train ride during weekday morning rush, the women-only car is an option.

If something happens — chikan response

  • Speak up loudly. "Chikan!" (痴漢) alerts everyone within earshot. Cultural pressure in Japan makes this effective; perpetrators usually freeze or flee.
  • Move cars, or report to station staff at the next stop. Station staff are trained for this. Chikan is a criminal offense — up to 6 months prison + ¥500,000 fine under Japanese law. [Deccan Herald]
  • Press the train's emergency button if needed — every car has one near the doors.

General train etiquette (low-stakes but worth knowing)

  • No phone calls on trains. Texting, scrolling, watching with headphones — all fine. Voice calls are explicitly discouraged via announcements.
  • Queue in marked lines on the platform — lines are painted on the floor at door positions.
  • Priority seats (marked with stickers showing pregnant, elderly, disabled, with-child icons) — offer them up if needed.
  • Eating on commuter trains: generally not done. Shinkansen and limited-express trains are the exception (ekiben culture — meals are bought and eaten on long-distance trains).

6. Drug laws — far stricter than US travelers expect

This extends the prescription medication note already in Logistics. Japan's drug laws are unforgiving and apply to US visitors with no flexibility.

  • Adderall, Vyvanse, and certain ADHD stimulants: illegal in Japan even with a US prescription. Bringing them in without a Ministry of Health Yakkan Shoumei import certificate can result in arrest and detention. The certificate process takes weeks — start months in advance if anyone in the family carries one. [US State Dept]
  • Marijuana products including CBD with any THC: illegal regardless of US state legality. CBD products that are legal in some US states have caused tourist arrests. Don't bring any cannabis-derived products.
  • Over-the-counter US cold/allergy medication containing pseudoephedrine (Sudafed, some Vicks formulations) is restricted in Japan. Use Japanese drugstore equivalents instead.
  • If someone is arrested on drug charges: Japan allows up to 23 days of detention without charge, conviction rates exceed 99%, and the US Embassy can provide a list of attorneys but cannot intervene in the legal process. (well-established features of the Japanese justice system; sourcing varies)

8. Lost-and-found — use the koban system

Japan's lost-and-found system is genuinely world-class. Knowing how to use it can be the difference between losing a wallet and getting it back same-day.

  • ~60,000 koban (police boxes) nationwide. Small green or blue-striped buildings with a police star symbol. Usually within a few blocks anywhere in Tokyo or Kyoto. [JRPass]
  • Tokyo lost-and-found return rates: phones ~83%, ID documents ~72%, wallets ~68%. The Tokyo Lost & Found Center processes ~9,000 items per day from koban plus ~7,000 from railways. [NotLost] [Tokyo Metropolitan Government]
  • If you lose something:
    1. If lost on a train, contact that line's lost-and-found counter first (each major operator has one — Tokyo Metro, JR East, JR Central, etc.)
    2. If lost on the street or in a shop/restaurant, go to the nearest koban and fill out a Lost Item Report (遺失物届, ishitsu-butsu todoke). Forms are available in English. [GaijinPot]
    3. You'll get a report number — keep it. Police will contact you if the item is recovered. Items are held for 90 days before disposal.
  • Act within ~7 days — that's the best window before items transfer from railway operators to the central police lost-and-found center.
  • If you find something: hand it to the nearest koban officer or station staff. Japanese culture takes this seriously; foreigners doing the same is appreciated.

For phones, passports, or wallets specifically: act fast, file the report at a koban (not just the railway counter), and recovery rates are remarkable.

Why Ninja WiFi is wrong for this trip (despite popularity)

  • 5-device limit is too tight. Your typical group during the day: 2–3 iPhones + iPad + occasionally a MacBook = 4–5 devices, right at the cap. Add a 5th person joining the group and you exceed it.
  • 9-hour battery doesn't survive a Disney day. Full Disney days run 14+ hours (7am leave hotel → 10pm park close, with midday break). Even with the included backup battery, you'd be running the second battery during evening park time. Tight.
  • "Excessive use" throttling possible. Three TikTok-posting teenagers tethered through one router can hit Ninja's threshold (unspecified but real).
  • Highest cost. ~$28 more than Japan Wireless for less capacity and worse battery.
  • The one thing Ninja is genuinely better at: airport counter coverage at every Narita/Haneda terminal. But not worth the trade-offs above.

What about 5G?

Japan Wireless and Sakura Mobile use 4G LTE. Ninja WiFi has a 5G option (NA02 unit) on premium plans only. For your family's use case, 4G LTE is genuinely indistinguishable from 5G.

  • 4G LTE in urban Tokyo/Kyoto delivers 30–80 Mbps download, 10–30 Mbps upload
  • TikTok scrolling: instant on 4G
  • TikTok 60-second clip upload: 5–15 seconds on 4G — fast enough that 5G wouldn't be perceptible
  • Instagram Stories, FaceTime, web browsing: identical experience on 4G vs 5G
  • 5G genuinely helps only for: 4K video upload from professional cameras (not your scenario), cloud gaming (not your scenario), multiple simultaneous 4K streams to multiple devices (use hotel WiFi)

Mobal's 5G eSIMs tested at 165 Mbps in early 2026, but for tourist social media use the speed premium isn't perceptible. [Tokyo Cheapo eSIM testing] Skip 5G for this trip.
